The **Air Quality Index (AQI)** is a standardized measurement used by the EPA and environmental agencies worldwide to communicate how clean or polluted the air is. It translates complex pollutant concentration data into a simple 0-500 scale that anyone can understand — the higher the number, the greater the health concern.

Understanding AQI is one of the most practical things you can do to protect your family's health. Whether you live near a highway, in a wildfire-prone region, or simply want to know when it is safe to exercise outdoors, AQI gives you the answer in seconds.

## How AQI Is Calculated

The AQI tracks five major pollutants regulated under the Clean Air Act:

- **Ground-level ozone (O3)** — Formed when sunlight reacts with vehicle and industrial emissions
- **Particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10)** — Fine and coarse particles from combustion, dust, and smoke
- **Carbon monoxide (CO)** — Primarily from vehicle exhaust and fuel combustion
- **Sulfur dioxide (SO2)** — From power plants and industrial processes
- **Nitrogen dioxide (NO2)** — From vehicles and power generation

Each pollutant is measured independently, and the **highest individual reading becomes the overall AQI value** for that location. For example, if PM2.5 reads 120 and ozone reads 85, the reported AQI is 120.

## AQI Levels and What They Mean

| AQI Range | Color | Level of Concern | Health Implications |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|---------------------|
| 0-50 | Green | Good | Air quality is satisfactory with little or no risk. |
| 51-100 | Yellow | Moderate | Acceptable, but sensitive individuals may experience mild effects. |
| 101-150 | Orange | Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups | Children, elderly, and those with respiratory conditions should limit prolonged outdoor exertion. |
| 151-200 | Red | Unhealthy | Everyone may begin to experience health effects. Sensitive groups face serious risk. |
| 201-300 | Purple | Very Unhealthy | Health alert — everyone is likely to be affected. Limit all outdoor activity. |
| 301-500 | Maroon | Hazardous | Emergency conditions. Stay indoors with windows sealed and air purifiers running. |

**The critical threshold is 100.** Below 100, air quality is generally acceptable for most people. Above 100, vulnerable populations need to take precautions.

## Who Is Considered "Sensitive"?

The EPA defines sensitive groups as people with:

- **Asthma or COPD** — Pollutants trigger airway inflammation and attacks
- **Heart disease** — PM2.5 particles enter the bloodstream and stress the cardiovascular system
- **Children under 14** — Their lungs are still developing, and they breathe more air per pound of body weight
- **Adults over 65** — Reduced immune response and higher rates of existing conditions
- **Pregnant women** — Pollution exposure is linked to preterm birth and low birth weight

If you or a family member falls into any of these groups, monitoring AQI daily should be part of your routine.

## How to Check Your Local AQI

There are several reliable ways to monitor air quality:

1. **AirNow.gov** — The EPA's official AQI reporting site with real-time data and forecasts for every U.S. zip code.
2. **IQAir and PurpleAir maps** — Crowdsourced sensor networks that provide hyperlocal readings, often more granular than government stations.
3. **Weather apps** — Most major weather apps now include AQI readings on their daily forecasts.
4. **Home air quality monitors** — Devices that track indoor AQI, PM2.5, CO2, VOCs, humidity, and temperature in real time.

For the most actionable data, we recommend pairing an outdoor AQI check with an indoor air quality monitor. Outdoor readings tell you when to keep windows closed; indoor readings tell you whether your purifier and ventilation are doing their jobs.

## Why Indoor AQI Matters More Than You Think

The EPA estimates that indoor air can be **2 to 5 times more polluted** than outdoor air. Cooking, cleaning products, off-gassing furniture, and inadequate ventilation all contribute. On days when outdoor AQI is high, opening windows can make indoor air significantly worse — but keeping everything sealed without mechanical filtration allows indoor pollutants to accumulate.

A dedicated indoor air quality monitor gives you visibility into your home environment and helps you make informed decisions about ventilation, purification, and daily activities.
