## Common Indoor Air Pollutants

Before you test, it helps to know what you are looking for:

### Particulate Matter (PM2.5 & PM10)
Fine particles from cooking, candles, dust, pet dander, and outdoor pollution that infiltrates your home. PM2.5 (particles smaller than 2.5 microns) is the most health-relevant because it penetrates deep into lungs.

**Safe level:** Below 12 µg/m³ (24-hour average, per WHO guidelines)

### Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs)
Chemical gases released by furniture, paint, cleaning products, air fresheners, and building materials. Common VOCs include formaldehyde, benzene, and toluene.

**Safe level:** Below 500 ppb total VOCs

### Carbon Dioxide (CO2)
A byproduct of human respiration. High CO2 levels indicate poor ventilation, which means other pollutants are also accumulating.

**Safe level:** Below 1,000 ppm (outdoor air is ~420 ppm)

### Radon
A naturally occurring radioactive gas that seeps into homes through foundation cracks. It is the #2 cause of lung cancer after smoking.

**Safe level:** Below 4.0 pCi/L (EPA action level), ideally below 2.0 pCi/L

### Humidity
Not a pollutant itself, but incorrect humidity levels enable mold growth (too high) or worsen respiratory symptoms (too low).

**Ideal range:** 30-50% relative humidity

Consumer monitors give you real-time data and historical trends, making them ideal for ongoing monitoring and identifying patterns (like cooking spikes or overnight CO2 buildup).

### Option 2: Professional Testing

For specific concerns like mold, asbestos, or detailed VOC analysis, hire a certified indoor air quality professional. They use lab-grade equipment and can identify specific compounds a consumer monitor cannot.

**When to hire a professional:**
- Suspected mold contamination
- Post-renovation concerns (asbestos, lead paint)
- Unexplained health symptoms in your household
- Real estate transactions

### Option 3: DIY Test Kits

Radon test kits are widely available at hardware stores for $10-$30. Short-term tests (2-7 days) give a snapshot, while long-term tests (90+ days) provide a more accurate average.

## Interpreting Your Results

Once you have air quality data, here is what to do:

1. **High PM2.5?** — Run a HEPA air purifier, address cooking ventilation, reduce candle/incense use
2. **High CO2?** — Improve ventilation by opening windows periodically or upgrading your HVAC system
3. **High VOCs?** — Identify the source (new furniture, cleaning products) and increase ventilation
4. **High radon?** — Contact a radon mitigation professional to install a sub-slab depressurization system
5. **High humidity?** — Use a dehumidifier and improve bathroom/kitchen ventilation
