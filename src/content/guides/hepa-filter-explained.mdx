HEPA stands for **High-Efficiency Particulate Air**. It is a type of mechanical air filter that forces air through a fine mesh of randomly arranged fibers. These fibers trap harmful particles like dust, pollen, mold spores, pet dander, and smoke particles.

A filter must meet a specific standard to be labeled "HEPA": it must capture **99.97% of particles that are 0.3 microns** in diameter. This size (0.3 microns) is known as the Most Penetrating Particle Size (MPPS) — particles both larger and smaller are actually easier to catch.

## How HEPA Filters Work

HEPA filters use three mechanisms to capture particles:

### 1. Interception
Particles following the airstream come within one radius of a fiber and stick to it. This catches medium-sized particles effectively.

### 2. Impaction
Larger particles cannot follow the curved airstream around fibers. Their inertia causes them to crash directly into the fiber. This is how dust and pollen are primarily captured.

### 3. Diffusion
The smallest particles (under 0.1 microns) move erratically due to collisions with gas molecules (Brownian motion). This random movement increases the chance they will hit a fiber. This is why HEPA filters are actually **more** effective at capturing the tiniest particles than the 0.3 micron benchmark suggests.

## True HEPA vs. HEPA-Type: A Critical Difference

This distinction can mean the difference between clean air and wasted money:

| Feature | True HEPA | HEPA-Type / HEPA-Like |
|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| Efficiency | 99.97% at 0.3 microns | 85-99% (varies) |
| Standardized | Yes (DOE standard) | No standard |
| Allergen removal | Excellent | Unreliable |
| Price | Higher | Lower |
| Worth it? | Yes | No, for health purposes |

**Our recommendation:** Always buy purifiers with **True HEPA** or **H13 HEPA** filters. Avoid anything labeled "HEPA-type," "HEPA-like," or "HEPA-style" — these are marketing terms that do not guarantee the 99.97% standard.

## HEPA Filter Grades

Medical and industrial HEPA filters are graded by efficiency:

- **H10-H12:** Lower grades, 85-99.5% efficiency. Found in basic consumer products.
- **H13:** 99.95% efficiency. The most common grade in quality consumer air purifiers.
- **H14:** 99.995% efficiency. Used in hospital operating rooms and cleanrooms.
- **ULPA (U15-U17):** 99.999%+ efficiency. Used in semiconductor manufacturing.

For home use, **H13 is the sweet spot** — it provides hospital-grade filtration without the excessive price and airflow restrictions of H14 or ULPA filters.

## What HEPA Filters Remove

HEPA filters are effective against:

- **Pollen** (10-100 microns) — Captured easily
- **Mold spores** (3-40 microns) — Captured easily
- **Dust mite debris** (0.5-50 microns) — Captured effectively
- **Pet dander** (0.5-100 microns) — Captured effectively
- **Bacteria** (0.3-10 microns) — Most captured
- **Some viruses** (0.01-0.3 microns) — Partially captured (many attach to larger particles)
- **Smoke particles** (0.1-1 microns) — Captured effectively

HEPA filters do **NOT** remove:

- **Gases and odors** (VOCs, formaldehyde) — Need activated carbon
- **Carbon monoxide** — Need a CO detector
- **Radon** — Need mitigation system

This is why the best air purifiers combine HEPA with **activated carbon filters** for comprehensive air cleaning.

## How Often to Replace HEPA Filters

Replacement frequency depends on:

- **Air quality** in your area (urban, rural, near highways)
- **Usage** (hours per day the purifier runs)
- **Pets** in the home (pet hair clogs pre-filters faster)
- **Manufacturer recommendation** (typically 6-12 months)

Most quality purifiers have a filter life indicator. Don't ignore it — a clogged HEPA filter reduces airflow and can actually make air quality worse by becoming a breeding ground for trapped bacteria.
