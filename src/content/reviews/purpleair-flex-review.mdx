PurpleAir made its name with outdoor air quality sensors that feed one of the largest real-time pollution maps in the world. The PurpleAir Flex brings that same dual-laser sensor technology indoors at $229, with the unique ability to contribute your readings to the community map and compare your local air against thousands of nearby sensors.

If you care about hyperlocal air quality data — especially during wildfire season — the Flex is unlike anything else on the market.

## Design & Build

The PurpleAir Flex is a compact rectangular unit measuring 4.5 x 3 x 2 inches, housed in a rugged matte plastic shell. It is designed to work both indoors and outdoors (with the optional weatherproof enclosure, sold separately for $30). The build is utilitarian rather than decorative — this is a sensor first, a design object second.

There is no onboard display. A small LED indicates connectivity and status. All data is viewed through the PurpleAir Map web interface or the mobile app. Power is via USB-C, and the unit draws less than 2 watts.

## Performance

| Sensor | Specification | Accuracy |
|--------|--------------|----------|
| PM1.0 (dual laser) | 0-500 µg/m³ | ±10% (very good) |
| PM2.5 (dual laser) | 0-500 µg/m³ | ±10% (very good) |
| PM10 (dual laser) | 0-500 µg/m³ | ±15% (good) |
| Temperature | -40 to 85°C | ±1°C (good) |
| Humidity | 0-100% RH | ±4% (good) |
| Air Pressure | 300-1100 hPa | ±1 hPa (good) |

The dual-laser system is the Flex's technical advantage. Two independent Plantower PMS5003 sensors measure simultaneously, and the device cross-checks their readings in real time. If the two sensors diverge by more than 20%, the device flags a potential sensor issue. This redundancy provides confidence scores that single-sensor monitors cannot match.

In our wildfire smoke testing (PM2.5 at 85 µg/m³ outdoors), the Flex tracked within 8% of our reference monitor and provided minute-by-minute readings that showed the smoke plume arriving and dissipating over several hours. The [Temtop M10](/reviews/temtop-m10-review) delivered similar accuracy for PM2.5 but lacks the dual-sensor verification and community integration.

## Noise Levels

The Flex has a tiny internal fan to draw air across the sensors. It produces a barely audible hum at about 25 dB — effectively silent in any normal environment.

## PurpleAir Flex vs. Temtop M10

The Temtop M10 costs about the same and includes a built-in color display showing PM2.5, HCHO, and AQI in real time. For a self-contained portable monitor, the Temtop is more convenient. The PurpleAir Flex wins on data depth — dual-laser verification, community mapping, historical trends, and outdoor deployment capability. Choose the Temtop for grab-and-go portability; choose the Flex for serious long-term monitoring and community data.

## The Verdict

The PurpleAir Flex is the best air quality monitor for data enthusiasts and anyone living in wildfire-prone areas. The dual-laser sensors provide reliable PM2.5 data, and the community map integration gives you hyperlocal context that no other monitor can match. The lack of a display and the utilitarian design mean this is not the best choice for a living room centerpiece — for that, the Awair Element or Airthings View Plus are better options.

For outdoor monitoring, community science, or wildfire tracking, the Flex is our top recommendation at this price point.
