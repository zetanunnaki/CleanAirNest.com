## Continuous Monitor vs. DIY Test Kit: Which Do You Need?

Before diving into specific products, it is important to understand the two fundamentally different approaches to radon testing. Your choice depends on whether you need a one-time answer or ongoing monitoring.

**DIY test kits** ($10-$30) are passive devices — typically charcoal canisters or alpha track detectors — that you place in your home for a set period, then mail to a certified lab for analysis. Short-term kits measure over 2-7 days. Long-term kits measure over 90 days to a year. They give you a single result, and if that result is elevated, you know to take action. The EPA recommends starting with a short-term test, and if results come back at or above 4 pCi/L, following up with a long-term test or a second short-term test to confirm.

**Continuous digital monitors** ($150-$300+) are electronic devices that measure radon levels in real time, 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. They show you current readings, daily averages, weekly trends, and long-term exposure data. This ongoing tracking reveals patterns — like whether radon spikes during winter when your home is sealed tight, or drops when you open windows. Continuous monitors pay for themselves if you want year-round awareness, need to verify that a radon mitigation system is working, or simply prefer the convenience of never mailing in a test kit again.

**Our recommendation:** Start with a test kit if you have never tested your home. If results show elevated radon, or if you want ongoing monitoring and already plan to invest in air quality tracking, go with the Airthings View Plus.

## 2. Best Budget Option: DIY Radon Test Kits ($10-$30)

Not everyone needs or wants a $299 continuous monitor. DIY radon test kits are inexpensive, widely available, and endorsed by the EPA as a reliable method for determining whether your home has elevated radon levels. For many homeowners, a test kit is all you need.

### Short-Term Test Kits (2-7 Days) — $10-$20

Short-term kits typically use activated charcoal to absorb radon from the air. You place the open canister in the lowest livable level of your home (usually the basement or ground-floor room), leave it undisturbed for 2-7 days, then seal it and mail it to a certified lab. Results arrive within a week, usually via email.

**When to use a short-term kit:**
- You have never tested your home for radon
- You are buying or selling a home and need results quickly
- You want a quick check after installing a radon mitigation system

**Tips for accurate results:**
- Test in the lowest livable level of your home
- Keep windows and exterior doors closed as much as possible during the test (closed-house conditions)
- Place the kit at least 20 inches above the floor, away from drafts, high humidity, and exterior walls
- Do not disturb the kit once placed
- Follow the kit instructions precisely — timing matters

### Long-Term Test Kits (90-365 Days) — $20-$30

Long-term kits use alpha track detectors that accumulate damage marks from radon decay particles over months. Because radon levels fluctuate with weather, seasons, and home ventilation, a long-term test provides a more accurate picture of your actual annual exposure than any short-term test can.

**When to use a long-term kit:**
- Your short-term test came back between 2-4 pCi/L and you want a more definitive reading
- You want to measure your true annual average radon exposure
- You are monitoring a home with a radon mitigation system over time

### Where to Get Test Kits

You can purchase radon test kits from hardware stores (Home Depot, Lowe's), online retailers like Amazon, or directly from certified labs. Look for kits analyzed by labs that participate in national radon proficiency programs. Many state radon offices also offer free or discounted test kits — check with your state health department.

## Radon Buying Guide: What You Need to Know

### Understanding Radon Levels

Radon is measured in picocuries per liter (pCi/L). Here is how to interpret your results:

- **Below 2 pCi/L** — Low risk. No action needed, but consider re-testing every 2-5 years since levels can change.
- **2-4 pCi/L** — Moderate. The EPA recommends considering mitigation, especially if a long-term test confirms levels in this range. The WHO recommends action above 2.7 pCi/L.
- **4 pCi/L and above** — The EPA action level. Mitigation is strongly recommended. The higher the level, the more urgent the need.
- **Above 8 pCi/L** — High. Prioritize mitigation. Some radon professionals recommend expedited remediation at this level.

### Where to Place Your Radon Monitor

Radon enters homes from the ground, so concentrations are typically highest at the lowest level. Place your test kit or continuous monitor:

- In the **lowest livable level** of your home (basement, ground floor)
- In a room where you spend regular time, if possible
- At least **20 inches above the floor** (radon is heavier than air but mixes readily)
- **Away from exterior walls, windows, and doors** to avoid draft interference
- **Away from high-humidity areas** like bathrooms or near sump pumps

### Factors That Affect Radon Levels

Radon concentrations in your home are not static. Several factors cause them to fluctuate:

- **Season:** Radon levels are typically higher in winter when homes are sealed tight and the temperature differential between indoor and outdoor air creates a stack effect that draws soil gas upward.
- **Weather:** Barometric pressure changes, rain-saturated soil, and wind can all affect radon entry rates.
- **Home construction:** Slab-on-grade, basement, and crawl space foundations all have different radon entry pathways. Cracks, gaps around pipes, and sump pits are common entry points.
- **Ventilation:** Opening windows and running exhaust fans dilutes radon but is not a sustainable long-term solution in most climates.
- **Soil type:** Homes built on granite, shale, or phosphate-rich soils tend to have higher radon levels.

This variability is exactly why continuous monitors like the Airthings View Plus are valuable — they capture these fluctuations and show you the long-term average that determines your actual health risk.

### What to Do if Radon Is High

If your test results show levels at or above 4 pCi/L:

1. **Confirm the result.** Follow up a short-term test with a second short-term test or a long-term test. A continuous monitor can also confirm over 30+ days.
2. **Hire a certified radon mitigator.** Look for professionals certified by the National Radon Proficiency Program (NRPP) or the National Radon Safety Board (NRSB). Mitigation typically costs $800-$2,500 depending on your home's foundation type and local rates.
3. **The most common fix is sub-slab depressurization** — a pipe and fan system that pulls radon from beneath your foundation and vents it above your roofline. This method reduces radon levels by up to 99% in most homes and runs continuously with minimal energy cost.
4. **Re-test after mitigation** to verify that levels have dropped below 4 pCi/L. A continuous monitor is ideal for ongoing verification.
