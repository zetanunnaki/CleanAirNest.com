You can't improve what you can't measure. An air quality monitor tells you exactly what pollutants are in your home — and whether your air purifier, ventilation, or humidity control is actually working.

After testing seven popular monitors against reference-grade lab equipment, these are our top recommendations for 2026.

## What Should an Air Quality Monitor Measure?

Different monitors track different pollutants. Here is what matters most:

- **PM2.5** — Fine particulate matter. The single most important metric for general health. Measures smoke, dust, and cooking particles.
- **CO2** — Carbon dioxide. Indicates ventilation quality. High CO2 means stale air and poor cognitive performance.
- **VOCs** — Volatile organic compounds. Off-gassing from furniture, paint, cleaning products.
- **Humidity** — Too high (above 60%) enables mold. Too low (below 30%) dries out airways.
- **Radon** — Radioactive gas that causes lung cancer. Only some monitors detect this.
- **Temperature** — Comfort metric, also affects humidity.

## How to Use Your Monitor Effectively

Once you have a monitor, here is how to get the most from it:

1. **Establish a baseline** — Run the monitor for a week without changing habits. Note your typical PM2.5, CO2, and humidity levels.
2. **Identify pollution sources** — Cook a meal, vacuum, light a candle. Watch how each activity affects air quality and how long it takes to return to baseline.
3. **Test your purifier** — Turn your air purifier on and off while watching PM2.5. You should see a dramatic drop within 15-30 minutes.
4. **Monitor overnight** — Check morning CO2 levels. If they are consistently above 1,000 ppm, you need better bedroom ventilation.
5. **Track seasonal changes** — Pollen season, wildfire season, and winter (when homes are sealed tight) all affect indoor air differently.
